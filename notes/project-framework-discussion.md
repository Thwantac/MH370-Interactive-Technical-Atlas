# Project Framework Discussion

## Purpose of the Atlas

The MH370 Interactive Technical Atlas is intended to be an evidence-first technical archive.

Its purpose is not to promote a preferred theory, but to preserve the evidence, competing analyses, assumptions, uncertainties, and predictions in a structured way.

The atlas should allow future readers to ask:

- What evidence existed at each stage?
- Which interpretations were based on strong evidence?
- Which interpretations were speculative?
- Which analysts or methods made predictions closest to the eventual wreckage location?
- Which assumptions later proved correct?
- Which assumptions failed?
- Why did some methods perform better than others?

If MH370 is eventually found, the atlas should become a retrospective audit of the investigation and the competing technical methods.

## Core Principle

Preserve every serious claim, but never give every claim the same evidentiary weight.

A claim can be fascinating without being strong.
A claim can be weak but still worth documenting.
A claim can be official but still incomplete.
A hypothesis should never be accepted merely because it is emotionally compelling.

## Evidence Levels

The atlas uses evidence levels to separate source strength from narrative interest.

### Level A — Primary Official Evidence

Primary official evidence includes original or near-original evidence from formal investigations and official records.

Examples:

- Malaysian Safety Investigation Report
- ATSB operational search reports
- Official radar summaries
- Official SATCOM/Inmarsat records where available
- Recovered debris examinations
- Official air traffic control records
- Officially released primary documents from governments or investigation agencies

Use Level A for evidence that forms part of the formal investigation record or has direct official provenance.

### Level B — Derived Official or Technical Analysis

Level B includes official or highly technical analysis derived from primary evidence.

Examples:

- Inmarsat BTO/BFO analysis summaries
- ATSB search-area modelling
- Official drift modelling
- Official or contracted technical reports
- Search-zone probability modelling
- Formal analysis based on official radar, satellite, or debris evidence

Use Level B when the evidence is not the raw source itself, but a serious technical interpretation of it.

### Level C — Credible Independent Analysis

Level C includes serious independent technical work with transparent reasoning, assumptions, and source references.

Examples:

- Independent Group analyses
- Technical blog posts with reproducible calculations
- Peer-style critiques
- Alternative route modelling based on official data
- Independent drift, fuel, radar, or satellite modelling with clear assumptions

Use Level C when the analysis is non-official but technically serious and traceable.

### Level D — Hypothesis-Generating or Contested Evidence

Level D includes evidence streams that are interesting, potentially useful, but disputed or not yet validated.

Examples:

- WSPR-derived MH370 track claims
- Experimental radio-propagation interpretations
- Media-reported expert claims
- Non-official reconstructions with limited reproducibility
- Claims that may be testable but are not yet established

Use Level D when the claim may be worth preserving and testing, but should not be treated as established.

### Level E — Speculative or Unsupported Claims

Level E includes claims without strong traceable evidence.

Examples:

- Anonymous claims
- Sensational media claims
- Unsupported military satellite claims
- Conspiracy narratives without primary evidence
- Assertions that cannot be traced to data or credible analysis

Use Level E to archive claims only when they are historically relevant to the MH370 debate.

## Future Evidence and Declassification

The atlas should be designed to accept future evidence.

This may include:

- declassified military satellite records
- declassified radar records
- intelligence assessments
- government communications
- search-planning documents
- contractor records
- additional Inmarsat or aircraft technical records
- future wreckage-location evidence
- future debris examination evidence

Future military satellite or intelligence records should not automatically be treated as speculative merely because they were once secret.

If formally released with clear provenance, such records may become Level A or Level B evidence.

For example:

- A formally released original military satellite observation record may become Level A.
- A government summary of a classified observation may become Level B.
- A media claim about an unnamed military satellite source remains Level E unless the source becomes traceable.
- A technical reconstruction based on declassified material may become Level C or B depending on provenance and method.

The evidence level depends on provenance, transparency, and traceability, not on whether the source is civilian or military.

## WSPR Evidence Stream

WSPR stands for Weak Signal Propagation Reporter.

WSPR is an amateur-radio weak-signal propagation system. It was designed to test HF radio propagation paths, not to track aircraft.

The MH370 WSPR hypothesis suggests that a large aircraft may have disturbed or scattered HF radio paths, leaving detectable anomalies in historical WSPR data.

This is fascinating because it may offer a new form of retrospective signal analysis.

However, it is contested.

The central scientific question is:

Can a single aircraft reliably create a detectable and distinguishable signature in historical WSPR records, strong enough to reconstruct a flight path?

WSPR material should therefore be preserved, studied, and mapped, but kept separate from official evidence.

Recommended classification:

- WSPR method explanation: Level C or D depending on source quality
- WSPR claimed MH370 track: Level D unless independently validated
- WSPR critiques: Level C if technically argued and source-backed
- WSPR validation against known aircraft tracks: potentially Level C or higher if reproducible

Any WSPR-derived Google Earth layer should be labelled clearly as:

Experimental WSPR-derived claimed track — not official evidence.

## Homicide / Suicide Theory and SATCOM Handshakes

The atlas should preserve discussion of deliberate-disappearance theories without presenting them as established fact.

A key question is:

If a deliberate actor disabled normal tracking systems, why were the SATCOM handshakes still available?

Important distinctions:

- The SATCOM pings were not engine pings.
- They were not hardware-programmed into the engines.
- They were network log-on / handshake events from the aircraft satellite communications system.
- ACARS reports could stop while the broader SATCOM system could still later communicate.
- The later Inmarsat/BTO/BFO forensic analysis was not obvious GPS-style tracking.

Possible explanations, if a deliberate theory were true:

- The actor may have understood transponder, radio, and ACARS systems but not the forensic value of SATCOM handshakes.
- The actor may have disabled ACARS reporting but not fully depowered the SATCOM equipment.
- The SATCOM system may have lost power and later rebooted.
- Permanently disabling the SATCOM terminal may not have been simple from the cockpit.
- The final 00:19 UTC SATCOM event may have been an automatic consequence of end-of-flight electrical changes.

These points should be treated as interpretive analysis, not proof of intent.

## Mapping Philosophy

Google Earth layers should separate evidence categories.

Suggested layer structure:

- Official timeline
- Civil ATC / secondary radar
- Military radar
- SATCOM / Inmarsat evidence
- Search areas
- Debris finds
- WSPR experimental analysis
- Other hypotheses
- Future declassified evidence
- Notes and cautions

Official evidence, contested evidence, and speculative hypotheses must not be visually merged without clear labelling.

## Retrospective Audit After Discovery

If MH370 is found, the atlas should support a retrospective assessment.

Questions to answer:

- Which predicted location was closest?
- Which evidence stream was most predictive?
- Which assumptions survived?
- Which assumptions failed?
- Which models overfit the available evidence?
- Which weak signals were genuinely useful?
- Which persuasive narratives were misleading?
- Which official choices were justified by evidence available at the time?
- Which non-official analysts were closest, and why?

The final goal is not only to document MH370, but to understand how technical truth emerges from incomplete, uncertain, and contested evidence.
